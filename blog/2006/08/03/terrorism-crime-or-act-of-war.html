Terrorism: Crime or Act of War?

Aug 3rd, 2006

Much of the world is continuing to have trouble deciding if Islamic terrorism is a mere crime, or an act of war. This stems from the fact that while major terror groups usually have state sponsors, their actions are not carried out on behalf of any particular nation. Even so, the real problem is the liberal humanistic mindset that now dominates international decision making. It insists on seeing the world according to national boundaries, when in fact the enemy is spiritual, transcending current borders. This has started to change in both Israel and the US, though both nations continue to resist the notion that the world is experiencing a clash of civilizations or religions. Israel regularly and publicly decries “Palestinian” and Lebanese terrorism as acts of war, and in most cases begins to respond accordingly, though liberal elements at home and concerns over criticism abroad usually bring an abrupt end to any military counter-action. Up until September 11, 2001, the US fell into the category of treating terrorism as a crime, launching investigations into individual attacks rather than going to war against its enemy. But America’s Christian leader remained clear-headed that day, and openly acknowledged that a global battle between good and evil, light and dark was under way and that it was time for his nation to join the fight. However, both Israel and the US will continue to find themselves fighting an uphill battle so long as they act as apologists for Islam, the so-called “religion of peace,” rather than demanding accountability from the followers of Allah. They will continue to find themselves fighting an uphill battle until they fully recognize that this is a clash of civilizations that has been raging since the advent of Islam and before, and stop allowing modern political boundaries to have such impact on their response. And lastly, they will continue to find themselves fighting an uphill battle until they fully come to terms with the fact that this is a war, and as such can only have one of two outcomes - victory or defeat. There can be no negotiated middle ground.
